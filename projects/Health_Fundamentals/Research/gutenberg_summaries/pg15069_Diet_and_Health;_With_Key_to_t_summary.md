### 1. Key Health Claims

- **Fat is caused by food, and food only.** The author states that "food, and food only (sometimes plus alcohol) maketh fat," directly refuting the idea that water or air contribute to weight gain.
- **Overweight individuals are "fireless cookers."** They hold heat in due to their fat layer, making them more efficient at storing food as fat.
- **Excess weight reduces efficiency.** The author claims that "efficiency decreases in direct proportion as excess weight increases."
- **Overweight is often a result of overeating and underexercising**, not necessarily a disease, though some cases are linked to glandular issues.
- **Mental work does not require added nourishment.** The book states that eating excess calories while doing mental work without exercise hinders performance.
- **Underweight is often linked to worry and nervous tension.** The author argues that worry burns calories, raises blood pressure, and impairs digestion, making weight gain difficult.
- **It is unpatriotic to be fat during wartime.** The book frames excess fat as "hoarding food" that the nation needs, creating a moral imperative to reduce.
- **Reducing weight can make you look younger and live longer.** The author boldly asserts that reaching a normal weight will make one "look fully ten years younger and live twenty years longer."
- **A "balanced diet" is essential for health**, containing specific proportions of protein, fat, and carbohydrates, as well as mineral matter, water, and "vitamines."

### 2. Dietary Recommendations

- **The Calorie System:** The book's central recommendation is to count calories. It provides a "Key to the Calories" with extensive tables listing the caloric value of hundreds of foods.
- **For Weight Loss:**
    - Reduce daily intake by 500-1000 calories below one's maintenance diet.
    - Eat a balanced diet with 200-300 calories of protein.
    - Include plenty of low-calorie, watery vegetables and fruits for their mineral salts, vitamins, and cellulose (fiber).
    - Use mineral oil or agar-agar for constipation, as they have no food value.
    - Avoid "tasting" food while cooking and avoid "anti-fat" medicines containing thyroid extract, arsenic, or mercury.
- **For Weight Gain:**
    - Gradually increase daily intake by 500-1000 calories above maintenance.
    - Consume high-calorie foods like butter, cream, sugar, chocolate, and malted milk.
    - Drink milk between meals and before retiring.
    - Use cod liver oil or olive oil.
- **General Dietary Advice:**
    - **Thorough mastication (Fletcherizing)** is "one thing more important than another."
    - Eat a variety of foods, but not too many at one meal.
    - At least half of one's protein should come from the vegetable kingdom.
    - Do not pare potatoes before cooking; cook vegetables in a small amount of water and save the water for soups.
    - Avoid drinking while food is in the mouth.
    - Saccharine is not advisable for habitual use.
    - Alcoholic drinks are harmful, making "the fat fatter and the thin thinner."

- **Personal Responsibility and Willpower:** The author emphasizes that weight control is a matter of discipline and habit. She frames it as a lifelong commitment, comparing it to other essential habits like being neat or kind.
- **Patriotic Duty and Social Pressure:** The book strongly links weight loss to patriotism during World War I, suggesting that being fat is unpatriotic and that forming "Watch Your Weight" clubs is a way to support the war effort.

- **Rejection of Fad Diets and "Magic" Solutions:** The book explicitly warns against dangerous anti-fat medicines, drastic purges, and violent exercises, advocating instead for a scientific, calorie-based approach.
