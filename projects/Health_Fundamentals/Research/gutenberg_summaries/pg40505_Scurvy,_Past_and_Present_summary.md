### 1. Key Health Claims

- **Scurvy is a Deficiency Disease:** The central claim is that scurvy is not an infection or a toxin-based disease, but a nutritional disorder caused by a deficiency of a specific, unidentified food factor, which the author calls the "antiscorbutic vitamine" (accessory food factor).
- **Scurvy is Identical in Infants and Adults:** The book firmly establishes that infantile scurvy (Barlow's disease) and adult scurvy are the same disease with an identical pathogenesis, settling a long-standing medical debate.
- **Scurvy is Preventable and Curable:** The book asserts that scurvy can be reliably prevented and cured by the inclusion of specific fresh foods in the diet, primarily fresh fruits and vegetables.
- **Pasteurization and Processing Reduce Antiscorbutic Value:** A key claim is that commercial pasteurization, homogenization, and other industrial processing of milk significantly reduce its antiscorbutic potency, making it a potential cause of infantile scurvy if not supplemented.
- **Scurvy Increases Susceptibility to Infection:** The book claims that scurvy creates a marked susceptibility to secondary bacterial infections (e.g., furunculosis, nasal diphtheria, "grippe"), and that hemorrhages in advanced scurvy may be focal complications of these infections rather than purely nutritional in origin.
- **War and Famine are Directly Linked to Scurvy:** The text claims that war is inevitably associated with scurvy due to the resulting food deprivation, particularly the lack of fresh produce.

### 2. Dietary Recommendations

- **Antiscorbutic Foods (Preventative and Curative):**
    - **Fruits and Fruit Juices:** Citrus fruits (lemons, oranges) and their juices are highly recommended. Canned tomatoes are also noted as a potent antiscorbutic.
    - **Vegetables:** Fresh vegetables, especially **potatoes** (identified as a critical winter staple), **cabbage**, and **swedes** (rutabaga), are strongly recommended.
    - **Germinated Cereals and Pulses:** Sprouted grains and legumes are identified as having antiscorbutic properties.
    - **Fresh Meat:** Especially **raw meat**, is cited as an effective antiscorbutic, crucial for Arctic explorers who lack access to vegetables.
- **Foods to Avoid or Supplement:**
    - **Heated/Processed Milk:** The book warns against relying solely on pasteurized, boiled, sterilized, dried, or condensed milk for infant feeding without additional antiscorbutic supplementation.
    - **Dehydrated Vegetables:** These are identified as a cause of scurvy, as the drying process destroys the antiscorbutic factor.
    - **Proprietary Infant Foods:** The text links the rise of infantile scurvy to the use of artificial feeding and proprietary foods.
- **Specific Recommendations for Infants:**
    - Raw cow's milk is superior to heated milk for preventing scurvy.
    - If using pasteurized or processed milk, it must be supplemented with an antiscorbutic like orange juice or tomato juice.
    - A diet of "malt soup" and cereal is identified as being frequently associated with scurvy.
    - Breast milk is generally protective, but a nursing mother on a severely deficient diet could theoretically produce milk that leads to scurvy in her infant.

### 3. Evidence Cited

- **Historical Epidemiological Evidence:** The book extensively cites historical outbreaks of scurvy in armies (e.g., Civil War, Crimean War, World War), navies (e.g., Lord Anson's expedition), besieged cities (e.g., Paris, Kut-el-Amara), and institutions (prisons, asylums). The common factor in all was a lack of fresh fruits and vegetables.
- **Contrasting Voyages:** The success of Captain Cook (who used "sweetwort" and sauerkraut) in preventing scurvy is contrasted with the devastating losses of earlier explorers, providing a powerful natural experiment.
- **Clinical Observation:** The author cites his own clinical experience and that of others (e.g., Barlow, Neumann) showing that substituting raw milk for pasteurized milk cures infantile scurvy, and that adding orange juice cures the disease.
- **Experimental Animal Studies:** The work of Holst and Froelich on guinea-pigs is cited as the classic scientific proof that scurvy is a deficiency disease. Their experiments showed that as little as 1.0 g of cabbage could protect a guinea-pig, while dried or heated vegetables could not.
- **Negative Evidence (Refuting Other Theories):**
    - **Potassium Theory:** Experiments showed potassium salts could not cure scurvy.
    - **Citric Acid Theory:** Artificial "orange juice" made of salts, citric acid, and sugar failed to cure scurvy in guinea-pigs.
    - **Acidosis Theory:** Adding alkali did not cure scurvy, and potatoes (an alkaline-ash food) are a good antiscorbutic.
    - **Toxic/Bacterial Theory:** The author's own studies (with Torrey) showed no significant change in the intestinal flora of scorbutic infants or guinea-pigs, arguing against a putrefactive toxin. The fact that officers in armies are spared while enlisted men suffer is cited as evidence against an infectious origin.
- **Bacteriological Studies:** The author notes that most blood cultures from scorbutic patients are negative, and that when bacteria are found (e.g., a diplococcus), it is likely a secondary invasion, not the primary cause.

### 4. Core Themes

- **The "Vitamine" Revolution:** The book frames scurvy as a key example of a new class of nutritional disorders caused by a lack of "accessory food factors" or "vitamines," shifting focus away from calories, proteins, fats, and salts.
- **Historical Perspective:** A major theme is the long and tragic history of scurvy, showing how its cause was repeatedly discovered and forgotten before the modern scientific era. The book serves as a historical record of human suffering from a preventable disease.
- **The Impact of Modern Food Processing:** The text critically examines how industrialization of the food supply (pasteurization, homogenization, dehydration, canning) can inadvertently destroy essential nutrients, creating new health problems even while solving others (like bacterial infections from milk).
- **Scurvy as a Social and Military Problem:** The book emphasizes that scurvy is not just a medical issue but a social one, linked to poverty, institutionalization, war, and famine. It highlights the "hand-to-mouth existence" of humanity regarding its antiscorbutic food supply.
- **Latent Scurvy:** A recurring theme is the concept of "latent" or "rudimentary" scurvy, a subclinical form that is difficult to diagnose but increases susceptibility to other diseases and infections, making it a more widespread problem than the number of full-blown cases would suggest.
