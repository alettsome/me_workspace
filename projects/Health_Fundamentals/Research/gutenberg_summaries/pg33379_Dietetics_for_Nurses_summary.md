Based on the provided text from "Dietetics for Nurses" (Second Edition, 1922) by Fairfax T. Proudfit, here is a structured summary of its health and nutrition claims.

### 1. Key Health Claims

The book makes several foundational assertions about the relationship between diet and health:

- **Prevention over Cure:** The central theme is that the "keynote of to-day's work is prevention, rather than cure." Proper nutrition is the primary tool for building strong, healthy individuals and a great nation.
- **Diet Determines Health:** There is a very close relationship between "right food and good health," a fact being constantly demonstrated by scientific research.
- **Vitamines are Essential:** The book asserts that a diet adequate in proteins, fats, carbohydrates, and minerals will still fail without the inclusion of "vitamines" (vitamins). These are essential for growth, development, and protection against specific "deficiency diseases" like beri-beri, scurvy, and xerophthalmia.
- **Incomplete Proteins Need Supplementation:** Foods like gelatin and cereals contain "incomplete" proteins that lack essential amino acids. They must be supplemented with "complete" proteins (from milk, eggs, meat) to support growth and tissue repair.
- **Food Iron is Superior to Medicinal Iron:** The book claims that iron from food is assimilated and used for building hemoglobin much better than inorganic or synthetic (medicinal) iron. Medicinal iron likely acts by stimulation, not as direct building material.
- **Basal Metabolism as a Diagnostic Tool:** Fluctuations in the basal metabolic rate (higher in fevers, lower in myxedema) are recognized as a factor in diagnosing disease.

### 2. Dietary Recommendations

The book provides specific recommendations for foods, nutrients, and eating patterns:

- **The Adequate Dietary:** An adequate diet must include a proper balance of all six essential food substances: carbohydrates, fats, proteins, water, mineral salts, and vitamines.
- **Sources of Complete Proteins:** Milk, eggs, and meat are the best sources of "complete" proteins, containing all the essential amino acids for building and repairing tissues.
- **Sources of Vitamines:**
    - **Fat-Soluble A:** Butter, cream, whole milk, egg yolk, cod liver oil, and green leafy vegetables (spinach, lettuce, cabbage).
    - **Water-Soluble B:** Yeast, whole grains (especially the embryo), milk, eggs, nuts, and legumes (beans, peas).
    - **Water-Soluble C:** Fresh fruits (oranges, lemons, grapefruit, tomatoes) and fresh vegetables (spinach, cabbage, lettuce).
- **Infant Feeding:** For infants, the safest source of iron is from the mother during the prenatal period. After birth, formulas should be supplemented with vegetable and fruit juices rich in the "B" and "C" vitamines.
- **Cooking Methods:**
    - Avoid frying, especially for the sick and children, as it is difficult to control the temperature and can create irritating compounds (acrolein).
    - Avoid adding soda (alkali) to cooking water for vegetables, as it destroys the "Water Soluble B" vitamine.
    - For protein foods, use gentle heat (e.g., soft-cooked eggs) to improve digestibility.
- **Water Intake:** It is advisable to drink six to eight glasses of water a day under normal conditions, but excessive fluid intake should be avoided as it strains the stomach, kidneys, and heart.

### 3. Evidence Cited

The author grounds her claims in the scientific understanding of the early 20th century, citing specific researchers and experimental methods:

- **Calorimeter Experiments:** The book references the "Bomb Calorimeter" devised by Berthelot and adapted by Atwater and Blakesley to measure the fuel value of foods, establishing the "physiological fuel factors" (4-9-4 calories per gram for carbohydrates, fats, and proteins).
- **Vitamine Research:** The author cites key scientists in the discovery of vitamins:
    - **Dr. Hopkins (England):** Demonstrated that milk contains a substance essential for growth beyond known nutrients.
    - **Osborne, Mendel, McCollum, and Davis:** Discovered the growth-stimulating substance in butter fat and plant leaves.
    - **Dr. Funk:** Discovered the substance in rice polishings that cures beri-beri and coined the term "vitamine."
    - **Dr. Hess (New York):** Found cod liver oil to be a remedy for rickets.
    - **Chick and Hume (England):** Provided evidence that soda in cooking water harms the "B" vitamine.
- **Metabolism Studies:** The book references DuBois for findings on basal metabolism in various diseases and Von Noorden for the reduced food requirements of the elderly.
- **Protein Studies:** The author cites Sherman's work on the efficiency of different proteins (lactalbumin vs. egg albumin) and the role of mineral salts.

### 4. Core Themes

- **The Nurse as a Nutrition Educator:** The book is written to equip nurses with the scientific knowledge to "spread the good work intelligently" and "assist in raising the standard of health" in the community.
- **The Body as a Transforming Machine:** The human body is not a simple steam engine but a "transforming machine" that converts the dormant energy of food into active energy for work and heat.
- **The "New" Science of Vitamines:** A major theme is the recent and revolutionary discovery of vitamins. The book emphasizes that a diet can be perfect in calories, protein, and minerals but still fail without these "essential factors."
- **Individualized Nutrition:** The book stresses that no two individuals are alike. Food requirements must be adjusted based on factors like age, sex, weight, muscular activity, and physical condition (e.g., pregnancy, disease).
- **Food Preparation Matters:** The way food is cooked (heat, alkali, method) directly impacts its digestibility and nutritional value, particularly its vitamin content.
