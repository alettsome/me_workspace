### 1. Key Health Claims

- **Starvation is a safe and effective treatment for diabetes.** The authors assert that fasting for 2-4 days (and up to 10-11 days in obstinate cases) is not dangerous and is the fastest way to eliminate sugar from the urine (glycosuria).
- **Weight reduction is beneficial for obese diabetics.** The book claims that reducing a fat diabetic's weight (by 10-20 pounds) can significantly improve their ability to remain sugar-free on a moderate diet. Weight gain is actively discouraged for adults.
- **Protein and fat are as important as carbohydrates in causing glycosuria.** The authors argue that an excess of protein can cause sugar to appear in the urine, and an excess of fat can cause ketonuria (acidosis). Therefore, both must be carefully restricted and monitored, not just carbohydrates.
- **Acidosis (ketosis) during starvation is not necessarily dangerous.** The authors state that the appearance of acetone and diacetic acid in the urine during fasting is not a cause for alarm and that starvation does not typically induce diabetic coma. This is presented as a key insight from Dr. Allen's work.
- **Exercise improves carbohydrate utilization.** The book claims that diabetic patients can utilize carbohydrates better if they take a considerable amount of exercise.

### 2. Dietary Recommendations

- **Starvation Period:** No food is allowed. Only black coffee and whiskey (1 oz every 2 hours) are given to provide roughly 800 calories and keep the patient comfortable. Bouillon or clear soup can be substituted for whiskey.
- **Post-Starvation Diet (Gradual Reintroduction):**
    - **Stage 1 (Vegetable Days):** Start with "5% vegetables" (low-carbohydrate vegetables like spinach, celery, cabbage, asparagus, string beans) that are **boiled three times** to reduce their carbohydrate content by about half. The first day's carbohydrate intake should be no more than 15 grams.
    - **Stage 2 (Slow Increase):** Protein and fat are raised very gradually, but not at the same time. The diet is increased slowly to find the patient's tolerance limit without causing sugar to reappear.
    - **Stage 3 (Maintenance):** A stable diet is established, typically around 1500-2000 calories for adults. The goal is not to push the diet higher if the patient is doing well.
- **Macronutrient Guidelines:**
    - **Carbohydrates:** Strictly limited, starting as low as 15 grams per day and raised slowly.
    - **Protein:** Must be watched as carefully as carbohydrates. For adults, the goal is about 1 gram per kilogram of body weight.
    - **Fat:** Kept relatively low to prevent acidosis and weight gain. Most adults do well on about 30 calories per kilogram.
- **Specific Foods:**
    - **Recommended:** Thrice-boiled green vegetables, butter, cream, bacon, eggs, meat (weighed carefully), and specially formulated low-carbohydrate breads/muffins (using Lyster flour, soya meal, casoid flour, and washed bran).
    - **Discouraged/Cautioned:** Most "gluten" and "diabetic flours" are called "gross frauds" due to high carbohydrate content. High-protein foods like gluten flour must be used with caution.
- **Monitoring:** Patients are taught to test their own urine every other day. A "semi-starvation day" (150 grams of vegetables) once a week is recommended to maintain the margin of safety.

### 3. Evidence Cited

- **Clinical Authority:** The treatment is based on the work of **Dr. Frederick M. Allen** of the Rockefeller Institute Hospital, whose methods are described as "undoubtedly a most valuable treatment."
- **Case Reports:** The book provides detailed case reports (11 cases) from the Massachusetts General Hospital and Children's Hospital. These cases serve as the primary evidence, showing:
    - Patients who failed to become sugar-free on the "old method" (gradual carbohydrate reduction) succeeded with the starvation method.
    - Specific examples of sugar levels dropping from 5-6% to 0% after 1-2 days of starvation.
    - Examples of sugar reappearing when protein was raised too quickly (e.g., Case 1).
    - A fatal case (Case 9) of a child already in coma, used to show that the treatment is not a cure for terminal cases.
- **Reasoning:** The authors argue that the treatment saves time compared to the old method and that a moderate loss of weight is desirable for obese patients. They reason that controlling protein and fat is as crucial as controlling carbohydrates.

### 4. Core Themes

- **The "Allen Treatment":** The entire book is a practical guide to implementing Dr. Frederick M. Allen's revolutionary approach to diabetes management.
- **Precision and Measurement:** A central theme is the absolute necessity for the physician and patient to "think in grams." Success depends on carefully weighing food and calculating daily protein, carbohydrate, and fat intake.
- **Rejection of Old Methods:** The book explicitly contrasts the new "starvation treatment" with the old method of "gradually cutting down the carbohydrate," which it claims was often ineffective at eliminating the last traces of sugar.
- **Three-Stage Process:** The treatment is clearly structured into three stages: (1) Starvation to become sugar-free, (2) Gradual diet increase to find tolerance, and (3) Stationary maintenance diet.
- **Managing Acidosis:** A significant theme is the management of acidosis (ketosis). The authors argue that it is not a reason to stop starvation and that keeping fat intake low helps control it.
