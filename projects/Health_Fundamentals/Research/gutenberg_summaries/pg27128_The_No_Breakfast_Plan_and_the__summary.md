Here is a structured summary of the health and nutrition claims found in Edward Hooker Dewey's *The No Breakfast Plan and the Fasting-Cure*.

### 1. Key Health Claims

- **Disease is a curative process of Nature, not an enemy:** Dewey argues that symptoms of disease (fever, pain, aversion to food) are not attacks to be fought, but rather the body's own efforts to restore normal function. The physician's role is to be a witness and interpreter, not a combatant.
- **The brain is a self-feeding, self-charging dynamo:** The brain does not need food during acute illness. It maintains its structural integrity and functional clarity by absorbing the body's own tissues (fat, muscle, organs) as "predigested food." This explains why the mind remains clear even when the body is emaciated.
- **Forced feeding during sickness is harmful and barbaric:** When there is no desire for food (due to fever, pain, or acute illness), the stomach cannot digest it. Forcing food (especially milk, whiskey, and "predigested" foods) wastes vital brain energy on digestion, adds the burden of decomposing food to the disease, and actively hinders recovery.
- **Fasting cures chronic disease:** Protracted fasts (from 30 to 60 days) are claimed to cure conditions like melancholia, general dropsy, chronic alcoholism, asthma, severe indigestion, and nasal catarrh by allowing the brain to accumulate power and restore tone to the entire vascular system.
- **Drugs are largely useless and often harmful:** Dewey states that with rare exceptions (pain relief), drugs do not cure disease. He calls the faith in medicines a "superstition" and argues that most recoveries happen *in spite of* drug treatment, not because of it. He specifically condemns alcohol and morphine as destructive to digestive power and brain function.
- **Overweight (obesity) is a form of "dropsy of debility":** Excess weight is not a sign of health but of weakened blood vessel walls (loss of tone), leading to fluid retention. Fasting and reduced eating are the cure.

### 2. Dietary Recommendations

- **The No-Breakfast Plan:** The central recommendation is to skip breakfast entirely. Dewey argues that a night's sleep is restorative, not exhausting, and that the body does not need food in the morning. Eating breakfast wastes brain energy that could be used for the day's work.
- **Eat only when genuinely hungry:** Food should only be taken when there is a natural, keen "hunger relish," not just a "taste relish" for tempting foods. This often means eating only one or two meals per day.
- **Slow eating and thorough mastication:** Dewey endorses the "Fletcherizing" method (chewing food until it is liquid), as advocated by Horace Fletcher.
- **Avoid milk for the sick:** Dewey strongly criticizes the use of milk as a "liquid food" for invalids. He argues that milk forms tough curds in the stomach that are indigestible during illness.
- **Avoid alcohol and "predigested" foods:** Whiskey and other alcoholics are condemned as containing no living atoms and as benumbing the brain. Predigested foods are dismissed as useless.
- **Fruit is acceptable:** Dewey mentions fruit (apples, pears, peaches) favorably, though he notes his own history of overindulging in it.
- **Simple, plain food:** When hunger returns after a fast, simple foods like bread and butter are described as being supremely relished and easily digested.

### 3. Evidence Cited

- **Personal Experience:** Dewey's own recovery from chronic dyspepsia and nervous exhaustion by skipping breakfast is his primary piece of anecdotal evidence.
- **Clinical Case Studies:** The book is filled with detailed case histories from his own practice (e.g., the typhoid fever patient who fasted for 35 days, the boy who died after a 75-day fast from caustic potash, the woman with inflammatory rheumatism who fasted for 46 days).
- **Physiological Data:** He cites a table from Yeo's *Physiology* showing that nerve-centers lose 0% of their weight during starvation, while fat loses 97%. This is his "missing link" to explain the brain's self-feeding power.
- **Comparative Cases:** He contrasts the outcomes of patients treated by other physicians (with heavy drugging, milk, and whiskey) with his own patients (treated with fasting and no drugs), claiming his patients recovered faster and with less suffering.
- **Public Fasts:** He includes press accounts of famous long-term fasts (e.g., Mr. Cowen's 42-day fast, Miss Kuenzel's 45-day fast for melancholia) as evidence that prolonged fasting is safe and curative.
- **Logic and Analogy:** He uses logical arguments (e.g., "if the nerves to the stomach are cut, digestion stops, therefore digestion is a brain function") and analogies (the brain as a dynamo, the stomach as a machine) to support his claims.

### 4. Core Themes

- **Nature as the Sole Healer (Vitalism):** The most dominant theme is that the body's own vital force ("Nature") is the only true curative agent. The physician's job is to remove obstacles (food, drugs) and let Nature work.
- **Critique of Conventional Medicine:** Dewey launches a fierce, polemical attack on the medical establishment of his time, accusing it of barbarism, superstition, and ignorance. He sees the drug industry and the practice of forced feeding as a form of professional malpractice.
- **Brain-Centered Physiology:** Dewey's entire system is built on the idea that the brain is the source of all energy (vital power). Health is a function of brain power; disease is a function of brain exhaustion. Saving brain energy by not eating is the key to health.
- **The Danger of Overeating:** A recurring theme is that most people, including himself, eat far more than they need, driven by "taste relish" rather than true hunger. This over-taxes the brain and leads to chronic disease.
- **The Physician as a Moral and Physical Example:** Dewey argues that a physician must be a model of perfect health (no tobacco, alcohol, or chronic ailments) to be effective, as health is contagious.

### 5. Notable Quotes

1.  **On the role of Nature in healing:**
    > "The hygiene unfolded is both original and revolutionary: its practicality is of the largest, and its physiology beyond any possible question. The reader is assured in advance that every line of this volume has been written with conviction at white heat, that enforced food in sickness and the drug that corrodes are professional barbarisms unworthy of the times in which we live."

2.  **On the brain's self-feeding power (the core discovery):**
    > "Instantly I saw in human bodies a vast reserve of predigested food, with the brain in possession of power so to absorb as to maintain structural integrity in the absence of food or power to digest it. This eliminated the brain entirely as an organ that needs to be fed or that can be fed from light-diet kitchens in times of acute sickness."

3.  **On the folly of feeding the sick:**
    > "If the well need food to keep up the strength, the sick need it all the more; this is the logic that has been displayed upon this question. ... Since vital power is centred in the brain, do we need to feed, can we feed, for other than brain reasons? This physiology admitted, there is no other conclusion possible than that feeding the sick is a tax on vital power when we need all that power to cure disease."
