### Structured Summary of the Study Protocol

**1. Research Question/Hypothesis**

This study is a protocol for a systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA). The primary research question is: **What is the comparative effectiveness of various adjunct non-pharmacological interventions (added to standard care) on maternal and neonatal outcomes in patients with Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM)?** The study aims to synthesize evidence from existing randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to determine which interventions are most effective for improving specific health outcomes for both the mother and the newborn.

**2. Key Findings**

This is a **protocol** for a future study, so it does not present results. However, it outlines the specific health and nutrition-related outcomes that will be investigated. The key findings will be determined after the review is conducted. The protocol identifies the following key areas of interest:

- **Maternal Outcomes:** Cesarean section, pre-eclampsia, polyhydramnios, preterm birth, prolonged labor, and gestational hypertension.
- **Neonatal Outcomes:** Macrosomia (large birth weight), neonatal hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), neonatal hyperbilirubinemia (jaundice), congenital anomalies, Apgar scores, birth weight, birth length, and gestational age at birth.
- **Nutritional Interventions:** The protocol will specifically compare the effectiveness of different nutritional supplements (e.g., vitamin D, probiotics, omega-3 fatty acids) against each other and against other non-pharmacological interventions.

**3. Evidence Strength**

- **Study Design:** This is a **protocol** for a systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) of randomized controlled trials (RCTs). This is a high-level evidence synthesis method.
- **Methodology Quality:** The protocol is rigorous and pre-registered (PROSPERO ID: CRD42021271199). It specifies:
    - A comprehensive search of three major databases (PubMed, Embase, Scopus).
    - Use of the Revised Cochrane risk-of-bias tool (RoB 2) to assess the quality of included trials.
    - A pre-planned statistical analysis using the frequentist method for NMA.
    - Assessment of publication bias using comparison-adjusted funnel plots.
    - Grading of evidence quality using the GRADE approach.
- **Sample Size:** The sample size is not predetermined, as it depends on the number of eligible RCTs found during the search. The protocol notes that previous individual trials have been "underpowered (due to relatively small sample sizes)," which is a key reason for conducting this meta-analysis to achieve greater statistical power.

**4. Clinical Implications**

This protocol is a roadmap for a future review. Its potential clinical implications are significant:
- **Guideline Development:** The planned review aims to provide the "agglomerated statistical evidence" currently missing, which could inform future clinical guidelines for GDM management.
- **Informed Decision-Making:** The results could help clinicians and patients choose the most effective non-pharmacological adjuncts (e.g., specific supplements, exercise programs, or digital tools) to improve outcomes.
- **Personalized Care:** By comparing different interventions, the review could help tailor recommendations (e.g., identifying which nutritional supplement is best for reducing macrosomia risk).

**5. Limitations**

The protocol itself has several limitations that will also affect the final review:
- **Language Bias:** The search is limited to articles published in English, which may exclude relevant studies and introduce bias.
- **Heterogeneity:** The included interventions (e.g., different types of exercise, different digital apps) and outcome definitions may vary significantly across trials, making it difficult to draw firm conclusions.
- **Quality of Underlying Data:** The strength of the final review's conclusions is entirely dependent on the quality and number of existing RCTs. The protocol acknowledges that many previous trials are underpowered.
- **Publication Bias:** The protocol will assess for this, but the risk remains that studies with negative or null results are less likely to be published.
- **Protocol vs. Results:** This document is a plan. The actual findings and their strength cannot be assessed until the review is completed.
