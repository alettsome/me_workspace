### 1. Research Question/Hypothesis

This review investigates how to optimise micronutrient supplementation during pregnancy. It specifically examines:
- The health effects of key bioactive compounds (vitamins and minerals) on maternal and foetal outcomes.
- The complex interactions (synergistic, additive, and antagonistic) between micronutrients that can alter their bioavailability and efficacy.
- The need for reassessing current nutritional guidelines and establishing harmonised regulatory standards for dietary supplements across the EU.

### 2. Key Findings

- **High Prevalence of Deficiencies:** Iron deficiency complicates nearly 50% of pregnancies worldwide, and deficiencies in vitamin D, calcium, folate, vitamin B12, and iodine are common, contributing to adverse outcomes like gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, neural tube defects, and anaemia.
- **Critical Role of Specific Micronutrients:**
    - **Folic Acid:** Periconceptional supplementation can prevent up to 70% of neural tube defects.
    - **Iron:** Physiological requirements rise significantly in the third trimester (3.0–7.5 mg/day).
    - **Iodine:** Deficiency during pregnancy can cause irreversible foetal cognitive impairment; recommended intake is 200–300 μg/day.
    - **Vitamin B12:** Deficiency, especially in vegetarian/vegan women, increases the risk of neural tube defects and megaloblastic anaemia.
- **Micronutrient Interactions Matter:**
    - **Synergistic:** Vitamin C and B2 enhance iron absorption, supporting healthy haemoglobin levels.
    - **Antagonistic:** High-dose iron can dose-dependently reduce zinc uptake.
- **Supplement Gaps:** A study in Poland found that most prenatal supplements fail to meet recommended intake levels for individual nutrients.
- **Regulatory Need:** There is a lack of EU-wide harmonised maximum levels for vitamins and minerals in supplements, posing a risk of excessive intake.

### 3. Evidence Strength

- **Study Design:** This is a **narrative review** (not a systematic review or meta-analysis). It synthesises findings from multiple existing studies, guidelines (e.g., EFSA), and regulatory documents.
- **Sample Size:** Not applicable (review article).
- **Methodology Quality:** The review provides a broad overview but does not use a systematic, reproducible search strategy or formal quality assessment of included studies. Its strength lies in highlighting key interactions and regulatory gaps, but it does not present new primary data.

### 4. Clinical Implications

- **Personalised Supplementation:** Supplementation should be tailored to the specific physiological stage of pregnancy (first, second, or third trimester) and individual risk factors (e.g., vegan diet, known deficiencies).
- **Consider Bioavailability and Interactions:** Healthcare providers should consider not just the dose of a single nutrient but also how it interacts with others (e.g., iron and zinc). Combining iron with vitamin C can be beneficial, while very high iron doses may impair zinc status.
- **Food First, Supplements Second:** Supplements are not a replacement for a balanced diet. Total intake from all sources (food, fortified foods, and supplements) must be monitored to avoid exceeding safe upper limits.
- **Focus on High-Risk Groups:** Pregnant women following vegetarian or vegan diets should be specifically counselled on supplementing vitamin B12 and iodine.

### 5. Limitations

- **Narrative Format:** As a narrative review, it is subject to author selection bias and does not provide a quantitative synthesis of evidence.
- **Lack of Primary Data:** The article does not present new experimental or clinical trial results.
- **Geographic Focus:** While discussing global issues, the regulatory analysis is heavily focused on the European Union, which may limit direct applicability to other regions.
- **Generalisability:** The review highlights that individual factors (age, health status, genetics) affect nutrient metabolism, but it does not provide specific algorithms for personalisation.
