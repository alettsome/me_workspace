# Structured Summary: Dietary Fibre and Chronic Kidney Disease

## 1. Research Question/Hypothesis

This systematic review investigated whether dietary fibre intake (from food sources or fermentable fibre supplements) affects inflammatory markers, gut-derived uraemic toxins, nutritional status, kidney function, and gut–liver–kidney axis mechanisms in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD). The review aimed to address why dietary fibre remains underrepresented in CKD clinical guidelines despite growing evidence of its potential benefits.

## 2. Key Findings

- **Inflammation**: Higher fibre intake was consistently associated with reductions in interleukin-6 (IL-6), with some improvements in TNF-α. C-reactive protein (CRP) responses were more variable. Resistant starch (16–25 g/day for 4–8 weeks) showed the most reproducible anti-inflammatory effects in haemodialysis patients.

- **Uraemic Toxins**: Fibre-based interventions produced variable but selective reductions in p-cresyl sulphate (pCS), indoxyl sulphate (IS), and p-cresyl glucuronide (pCG). Effects depended on fibre type, toxin fraction (free vs. total), dialysis modality, and intervention duration.

- **Nutritional Status**: Fibre supplementation was consistently safe—albumin, body weight, BMI, and energy/protein intake were maintained or improved across studies. No evidence of protein-energy wasting was observed.

- **Kidney Function**: Most short-term trials showed stable eGFR, creatinine, and urea. Longer-duration or whole-diet interventions suggested possible renoprotective benefits, but evidence remains limited.

- **Gut–Liver–Kidney Axis**: Fermentable fibres consistently shifted gut microbiota toward saccharolytic fermentation, increasing short-chain fatty acid (SCFA)-producing taxa (e.g., *Faecalibacterium*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Lactobacillus*) and improving bowel transit.

- **Observational Evidence**: A large prospective cohort (110,412 participants) found an approximate 3% reduction in CKD incidence per 1 g/1000 kcal higher fibre intake. Lower fibre intake was associated with higher all-cause mortality risk in CKD patients.

## 3. Evidence Strength

- **Study Design**: Systematic review following PRISMA 2020 guidelines
- **Databases Searched**: PubMed, Scopus, Medline Complete (January 2006–December 2025)
- **Studies Included**: 45 human studies (observational and interventional)
- **Screening**: 4,467 records identified, 2,397 unique records screened, 118 full-text articles assessed
- **Methodology**: Narrative synthesis (Synthesis Without Meta-Analysis) due to substantial heterogeneity; risk-of-bias assessment using domain-based tools; PROSPERO-registered (CRD420251275865)
- **Quality Caveat**: Many studies had small sample sizes, short durations, and some had serious or critical risk of bias. Observational evidence restricted causal inference.

## 4. Clinical Implications

- **Modality-Specific Recommendations**:
  - **Predialysis CKD**: Inulin 10 g/day for 12 weeks or β-glucan ~3 g/day for 14 weeks for reducing inflammation and free toxin fractions
  - **Haemodialysis**: Resistant starch 16–25 g/day for 8–16 weeks for cytokine-mediated inflammation and pCS reduction
  - **Peritoneal Dialysis**: Inulin-type fructans 10 g/day for 12 weeks or FOS 20 g/day for supporting fermentative balance or managing constipation

- **General Guidance**: Dietary fibre intake ≥20–25 g/day should be encouraged where potassium tolerance allows, as part of personalised renal nutrition

- **Safety Considerations**: Gradual introduction required; monitor potassium, gastrointestinal tolerance, and energy/protein adequacy; use food-preparation techniques (soaking, discarding cooking water) to reduce potassium content of fibre-rich foods

- **Guideline Gap**: Fibre remains underrepresented in CKD dietary guidelines (including KDIGO 2024); the review recommends setting minimum fibre intake recommendations tailored to CKD stages

## 5. Limitations

- **Language bias**: Exclusion of non-English publications
- **Heterogeneity**: Wide variation in fibre types, doses, study designs, and outcome measures limiting comparability
- **Causal inference**: Several domains relied on observational evidence
- **Short durations**: Most trials were brief (weeks), limiting assessment of long-term kidney outcomes
- **Publication bias**: Most included trials were small and tended to report positive biomarker changes
- **Selective reporting**: Several studies provided only partial biomarker data or incomplete toxin-fraction reporting
- **Access limitations**: Some full-text publications were unavailable from the authors' institution
- **Single-reviewer screening**: Title/abstract and full-text screening performed by one reviewer (independently checked by a second)
