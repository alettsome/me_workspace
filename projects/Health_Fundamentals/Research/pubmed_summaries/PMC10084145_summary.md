# Structured Summary: British Dietetic Association Consensus Guidelines on Nutritional Assessment and Dietary Management of IBD

## 1. Research Question/Hypothesis

This study aimed to develop evidence-based consensus guidelines for the nutritional assessment and dietary management of patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including both Crohn's disease (CD) and ulcerative colitis (UC). The researchers investigated 17 research topics related to IBD and nutrition, seeking to establish best-practice recommendations where high-quality evidence is limited.

## 2. Key Findings

**Nutritional Assessment:**
- Disease activity significantly affects body composition in IBD, with sarcopenia common even in overweight patients
- BMI alone is an inaccurate marker of malnutrition; comprehensive assessment should include body composition and muscle function measures
- Handgrip strength is recommended as a practical, quick, and inexpensive assessment tool
- Clinical interpretation of serum micronutrients may be unreliable during active inflammation (CRP >20 mg/L for zinc, >10 mg/L for selenium, >5 mg/L for vitamin B12)

**Micronutrient Deficiencies:**
- Magnesium deficiency was found in 28.7% of CD patients and 22% of UC patients in remission
- Selenium deficiency was found in 61.7% of CD patients in remission
- Vitamin D deficiency is common in IBD patients
- Iron is an acute phase reactant; anaemia of chronic disease often co-exists with iron deficiency anaemia

**Dietary Management:**
- Exclusive enteral nutrition (EEN) for minimum 6 weeks may induce remission in mild-to-moderate CD, particularly when corticosteroids are contraindicated
- EEN for 4-12 weeks may induce remission in CD patients with inflammatory strictures (85% achieved remission)
- Pre-surgical EEN (mean 6 weeks) reduced post-surgical hospitalisation by 2 days and lowered infectious complications (13% vs 29%)
- No evidence supports whole food diets to induce remission in active IBD
- Fibre intake is typically low in IBD patients and should be assessed
- Calcium and iron intake should be assessed as patients may not meet recommended intakes

**Probiotics and Supplements:**
- Specific probiotics alongside usual medication may support remission induction in mild UC (moderate quality evidence)
- No evidence for probiotics in CD for induction or maintenance of remission
- Curcumin supplementation with mesalamine may improve clinical response in active UC
- Insufficient evidence for fish oil supplements to reduce disease activity

## 3. Evidence Strength

- **Study Design:** Systematic reviews with Delphi consensus methodology (eDelphi online platform)
- **Sample Size:** 23,824 non-duplicated papers screened; 167 critically appraised; 55 participants completed 3 rounds of voting
- **Methodology Quality:** GRADE framework used for evidence quality assessment; 14 GRADE statements and 42 practice statements achieved 80% consensus
- **Expert Panel:** Included healthcare professionals (dietitians, gastroenterologists) and patients with IBD
- **Voting Process:** Three rounds of anonymous voting with iterative refinement based on feedback

## 4. Clinical Implications

**For Healthcare Providers:**
- Implement comprehensive nutritional assessment beyond BMI, including body composition and muscle function
- Use MUST (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool) for malnutrition risk screening, but recognise its limitations in IBD
- Interpret serum micronutrient levels cautiously during active inflammation
- Consider EEN as a therapeutic option for CD, particularly when corticosteroids are contraindicated
- Discourage self-directed non-evidence-based exclusion diets that may promote nutritional deficiency

**For Patients:**
- Include a wide range of fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and wholegrains (except in stricturing disease)
- Limit red and processed meat to no more than twice weekly
- Consider vitamin D supplementation year-round, especially if deficient
- No need to restrict fibre intake except in stricturing disease
- Patients on steroids should receive calcium and vitamin D supplementation

**For Clinical Practice:**
- Multidisciplinary team involvement is vital for comprehensive IBD care
- Dietary counselling should address energy, macronutrient, and micronutrient intake
- Oral nutritional supplements may improve nutritional status in malnourished patients

## 5. Limitations

- **Evidence Quality:** Many GRADE statements were rated as "very low quality" due to limited RCT data
- **Sample Size:** Only 55 of 114 initial participants completed all three voting rounds
- **Geographic Scope:** Guidelines developed primarily within UK context (British Dietetic Association)
- **Heterogeneity:** Wide variation in study designs, interventions, and outcome measures across included studies
- **Limited RCT Data:** For many dietary interventions, only observational or case series data were available
- **Patient Population:** Most studies focused on adults; paediatric considerations were not addressed
- **Publication Bias:** Potential for unpublished negative results not included in systematic reviews

## 6. Notable Quotes

1. *"Disease activity affects body composition in inflammatory bowel disease; therefore, it is desirable to assess nutritional status longitudinally using body mass index in combination with assessments of body composition and/or muscle function."* (96.2% agreement)

2. *"In inflammatory bowel disease, clinical interpretation of some serum micronutrients may be unreliable during inflammation."* (96.2% agreement)

3. *"Discourage self-directed non-evidence-based exclusion diets as they can promote nutritional deficiency and low diet quality. Many dietary components have been associated with IBD, however, there is no evidence to support restriction of any one dietary component."*
