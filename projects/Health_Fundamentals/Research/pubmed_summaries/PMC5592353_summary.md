# Text messaging (SMS) as a tool to facilitate weight loss and prevent metabolic deterioration in clinically healthy overweight and obese subjects: a randomised controlled trial

Here is a structured summary of the article "Text messaging (SMS) as a tool to facilitate weight loss and prevent metabolic deterioration in clinically healthy overweight and obese subjects: a randomised controlled trial" (PMC5592353).

### 1. Research Question/Hypothesis
The study investigated whether sending bi-weekly SMS text messages (containing nutritional information and behavioural encouragement) to clinically healthy overweight and obese adults could facilitate significant reductions in weight, waist circumference (WC), and body mass index (BMI), as well as improve metabolic markers (lipid profile, fasting glucose, and insulin levels) compared to standard lifestyle advice alone.

### 2. Key Findings
- **Anthropometric Changes (Weight, BMI, WC):** The intervention group showed a statistically significant mean weight loss of **2.4%** (approximately 3.4 kg) and a **4.8% reduction in waist circumference** over one year. In contrast, the control group (which received only initial advice) showed a non-significant increase in weight and BMI.
- **Clinically Significant Weight Loss:** A higher proportion of the SMS group achieved meaningful weight loss targets: **8%** lost at least 10% of their body weight (vs. 0% in controls), and **21%** lost at least 5% (vs. 13% in controls).
- **Metabolic Markers:** Despite the weight loss, the study found **no statistically significant between-group differences** in total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, triglycerides, or insulin resistance (HOMA-IR). Fasting glucose levels increased slightly in both groups, with no difference attributable to the SMS intervention.

### 3. Evidence Strength
- **Study Design:** Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT).
- **Sample Size:** 123 participants completed the study (63 intervention, 60 control) out of 129 recruited. The sample was drawn from a single primary care centre in Riga, Latvia.
- **Methodology Quality:** The study was a 12-month trial with a low dropout rate (<10%). Both groups received identical initial lifestyle and dietary advice. The intervention group received additional automated SMS messages every two weeks. However, the study was not blinded (participants knew they were receiving texts), and the sample size was calculated based on an expected 10% weight loss, which the intervention did not achieve.

### 4. Clinical Implications
- **Low-Cost Support Tool:** SMS messaging is a feasible, inexpensive, and scalable tool for primary care settings to support modest weight loss (2-3%) and waist circumference reduction in high-risk populations.
- **Complementary Strategy:** While SMS alone did not reverse metabolic deterioration (glucose/lipids), it helped prevent the weight gain observed in the control group. It can be used as a "nudge" to reinforce dietary and physical activity advice between clinical visits.
- **Target Population:** The results are most applicable to clinically healthy, overweight/obese adults (aged 30-45) without existing cardiovascular disease or diabetes, particularly in high-CVD-risk regions.

### 5. Limitations
- **Modest Effect Size:** The intervention did not reach the target 10% weight loss, and the overall weight loss was modest (2.4%).
- **No Metabolic Improvement:** The study failed to show significant improvements in lipid profiles or glucose metabolism, which are key drivers of cardiovascular risk.
- **Generalizability:** The study was conducted in a single urban health centre in Latvia with an established patient-physician relationship, limiting generalizability to other populations or settings.
- **No Interim Monitoring:** There were no check-up visits at 3 or 6 months, making it difficult to assess the trajectory of weight loss or compare results with shorter-term studies.
